Abstract

From the early days of functional neuroimaging, researchers have leveraged peripheral physiological sensing and concurrent use of the technology to explore brain-heart connections. From demonstrating simple correlations between parameters such as heart rate and fear-implicated brain regions such as the amygdala to very recent prediction of heart rate and skin conductance using fMRI data, a vast amount of literature has established that at least in certain contexts, limbic brain activity and certain peripheral physiological features are significantly intertwined. Yet, translating this scientific result to an application- oriented view of using peripheral biosignals to estimate brain activity has not been accomplished. This project focused on this regression problem to lay the framework for future research in this area. Due to data limitation constraints, however, we sought to find clever ways to summarize our plentiful physiological data to better map to brain activity. One of these ways was inspired by LFADS, in that we hypothesized that latent autonomic nervous system state dynamics drive the observable changes in peripheral physiological features observed, and hence, by constructing such a nonlinear latent state model, we may be able to use lower dimensional factors to map to brain activity. To this end, we experimented with a variety of subject-independent approaches to this regression problem using LSTMs, support vector regression, ridge regression, etc. Concurrently, we implemented a many-to-one LSTM as a fully supervised approach for comparison. Upon observing unsatisfactory results from all subject- independent approaches, we spent the remaining couple weeks of the semester stepping back and analyzing the data using simpler correlation analysis, as well as by experimenting with subject-specific modeling. These results were more promising, with statistically significant correlations being discovered and positive R squared values being produced for subject-specific regressions. These results give us hope for fruitful research in this area, even if the exact ideas laid out herein prove intractable.
